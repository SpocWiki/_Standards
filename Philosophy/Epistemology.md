# [[Epistemology]] 

#is_/same_as :: [[../WikiData/WD~Epistemology,9471|WD~Epistemology,9471]] 

Epistemology derives from the Greek ἐπιστήμη (_epistēmē_, "knowledge") 
and λόγος (_logos_, "reasoned account"). 
It constitutes one of the four classical branches of philosophy alongside 
metaphysics, logic, and ethics, and provides the foundational framework 
through which all other fields evaluate their claims to knowledge.

## #has_/text_of_/abstract 

> **Epistemology** is the branch of philosophy that examines the nature, origin, and limits of knowledge. 
> Also called theory of knowledge, it explores different types of knowledge, such as 
> - propositional knowledge about facts, 
> - practical knowledge in the form of skills, and 
> - knowledge by acquaintance as a familiarity through experience. 
> 
> Epistemologists study the concepts of belief, truth, 
> and justification to understand the nature of knowledge. 
> To discover how knowledge arises, they investigate sources of justification, 
> such as perception, introspection, memory, reason, and testimony.
>
> The school of skepticism questions the human ability to attain knowledge 
> while fallibilism says that knowledge is never certain. 
> 
> Empiricists hold that all knowledge comes from sense experience, 
> whereas rationalists believe that some knowledge does not depend on it. 
> 
> Coherentists argue that a belief is justified if it coheres with other beliefs. 
> Foundationalists, by contrast, maintain that the justification of basic beliefs 
> does not depend on other beliefs. 
> Internalism and externalism disagree about 
> whether justification is determined solely by mental states or also by external circumstances.
>
> Separate branches of epistemology are dedicated to knowledge found in specific fields, like scientific, mathematical, moral, and religious knowledge. Naturalized epistemology relies on empirical methods and discoveries, whereas formal epistemology uses formal tools from logic. Social epistemology investigates the communal aspect of knowledge and historical epistemology examines its historical conditions. Epistemology is closely related to psychology, which describes the beliefs people hold, while epistemology studies the norms governing the evaluation of beliefs. It also intersects with fields such as decision theory, education, and anthropology.
>
> Early reflections on the nature, sources, and scope of knowledge are found in ancient Greek, Indian, and Chinese philosophy. The relation between reason and faith was a central topic in the medieval period. The modern era was characterized by the contrasting perspectives of empiricism and rationalism. Epistemologists in the 20th century examined the components, structure, and value of knowledge while integrating insights from the natural sciences and linguistics.
>
> [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology) 

## Definition and Scope

Epistemology investigates the **nature, sources, structure, and limits** of knowledge. 
Its central questions are:  
- What is knowledge, and how does it differ from mere belief?  
- What are knowledge's sources and its limits?  
- What makes a belief _justified_?

### Structural Dimensions of the Discipline

|Dimension|Central Question|Representative Debate|
|---|---|---|
|Nature of Knowledge|What distinguishes knowledge from belief?|JTB vs. post-Gettier analyses|
|Sources|Where does knowledge originate?|Rationalism vs. Empiricism|
|Justification Structure|How do beliefs acquire warrant?|Foundationalism vs. Coherentism vs. Reliabilism|
|Scope & Limits|What can we know?|Skeptical challenges|
|Epistemic Agency|Who is the knower?|Virtue Epistemology|

## Classical; Justified True Belief (JTB)

The dominant classical definition, 
traceable to Plato's _Theaetetus_ (c. 369 BCE) and _Meno_ (c. 380 BCE), holds that 
knowledge is **Justified True Belief**:

Knowledge(agent,P)  ≡  Belief(agent,P)  ∧  Truth(P)  ∧  Justified(agent,P) 

The analysis remained the dominant framework for approximately **2,350 years** 
(c. 380 BCE – 1963 CE) and continues to serve as the standard starting point for 
~90 % of contemporary epistemological literature.

### The Three Necessary and Jointly Sufficient Conditions

| Condition         | Description                          | Failure Mode                          | Example of Failure             |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| **Belief**        | Agent sincerely assents to P         | Mere conjecture, **wishful thinking** | "I hope it's raining"          |
| **Truth**         | P corresponds to reality             | Justified but **false belief**        | Ptolemaic astronomy            |
| **Justification** | Agent has adequate epistemic warrant | **Accidentally true** guess           | Correctly guessing a coin flip |

### Internalism vs. Externalism on Justification

Two major meta-level positions on what _justification_ requires:

| Position         | Core Claim                                                     | Prevalence (PhilPapers 2020, n ≈ 1,785) | Key Proponents                     |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| **Internalism**  | Justifying factors must be cognitively accessible to the agent | ~27 %                                   | Chisholm, BonJour, Conee & Feldman |
| **Externalism**  | Justifying factors may be external to the agent's perspective  | ~44 %                                   | Goldman, Armstrong, Nozick         |

## The Gettier Problem

Edmund Gettier (1963) published a **3-page paper** — "Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?" (_Analysis_, Vol. 23, No. 6, pp. 121–123, 1963-06) — 
that provided decisive counterexamples to JTB.

### The Stopped Clock Case

> An agent glances at a wall clock and reads 3:15. The belief "It is 3:15" is:  
> 1. held sincerely (**Belief** ✓), 
> 2. true — it happens to be 3:15 (**Truth** ✓), and 
> 3. well-supported by normal clock-reading procedure (**Justification** ✓).  
> Yet the **clock stopped exactly 12 hours prior**. 
> Intuitively, the agent does **not** _know_ it is 3:15.

### Logical Structure

The Gettier structure can be formalized as:

GettierCase(P)  ≡  JTB(agent,P)  ∧  ¬  CausalLink(Justification,Truth(P)) 

### Proposed Responses to Gettier

| Response                  | Added Condition                                                    |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **No False Lemmas**       | Justification chain must pass through no false intermediate belief |
| **Causal Theory**         | Fact must causally produce the belief                              |
| **Reliability Condition** | Belief must be formed by a reliable process                        |
| **Safety Condition**      | Belief could not _easily_ have been false                          |
| **Virtue Epistemology**   | Belief must be attributable to intellectual virtue                 |
| **Contextualism**         | "Knows" standards vary by conversational context                   |

## Theories of Justification

The classical **Regress Problem** drives the taxonomy:

If B1 is justified by B2, and B2 by B3… then: 

|Regress Terminus|Theory|
|---|---|
|Chain terminates at non-inferred beliefs|**Foundationalism**|
|Chain loops (mutual support)|**Coherentism**|
|Chain continues infinitely|**Infinitism**|
|No chain needed (external process)|**Reliabilism**|

### Summary Comparison

| Theory          | Regress Solution       | Belief Structure     | Key Proponents              |
| --------------- | ---------------------- | -------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Foundationalism | Terminates             | Hierarchical pyramid | Descartes, Chisholm, Ayer   |
| Coherentism     | Loops / mutual support | Web / network        | BonJour, Neurath, Davidson  |
| Reliabilism     | Bypassed externally    | Causal chain         | Goldman, Armstrong, Dretske |
| Infinitism      | Infinite non-repeating | Linear (unbounded)   | Klein, Peijnenburg          |

### Foundationalism

**Basic beliefs** possess **non-inferential** justification; 
all other (derived) beliefs obtain their justification from them:

Justification(B_derived)≤Justification(B_basic)×TransmissionFactor


#### Variants

|Variant|Basic Belief Character|Representative Thinker|
|---|---|---|
|Cartesian / Classical|Infallible, indubitable|Descartes (_Meditations_, 1641)|
|Modest Foundationalism|Fallible but prima facie justified|Chisholm (1966), Pryor (2000)|
|Sense-Data Foundationalism|Phenomenal experience as base|Ayer, Russell|

#### Principal Objections

- **The Sellarsian Myth of the Given**: No belief is epistemically self-justifying (Sellars, 1956).
- **Arbitrariness**: Why should basic beliefs count as _justified_ if they receive no inferential support?

### Coherentism

No beliefs are privileged; justification is a function of **mutual coherence** across the entire belief web:

CoherenceScore(B)=f ⁣(Consistency,  ExplanatoryPower,  Simplicity,  Comprehensiveness)

#### Principal Objections

|Objection|Description|
|---|---|
|**Isolation / Input Problem**|A fully coherent belief set could be entirely disconnected from reality|
|**Alternative Coherent Systems**|Many mutually incompatible belief systems can be internally coherent|

### Reliabilism

Reliabilism (Goldman, 1979) is an **externalist** theory: 
a belief is justified iff it is produced by a cognitive process 
with a sufficiently high truth-yield:

Justification(B)=True    ⟺    ReliabilityRate(CognitiveProcess)≥θ 

Where:

ReliabilityRate=TrueBeliefsProduced / TotalBeliefsProduced
#### Variants

|Variant|Focus|Proponent|
|---|---|---|
|Process Reliabilism|Type of cognitive process (perception, memory, inference)|Goldman (1979)|
|Indicator Reliabilism|Reliable indicator relations between states of affairs and beliefs|Dretske (1981)|
|Proper Functionalism|Faculties functioning _as designed_ in an appropriate environment|Plantinga (1993)|

#### Principal Objection

The **Generality Problem**: There is no principled criterion for individuating the _type_ of cognitive process whose reliability is to be assessed.

### Infinitism

Peter Klein (1999–2007) argues that justification requires an infinite, non-repeating chain of reasons. Agents need not _complete_ the chain — they must merely have an _unbounded potential_ to provide further reasons:

JustificationChain={R1,R2,R3,…}   where ∀ i≠j:  Ri≠Rj   and ∣chain∣→∞

#### Principal Objections

- **Human Finitude**: Finite minds cannot actually traverse an infinite chain.
- **Completion Requirement**: Justification may require actually having reasons, not merely potential ones.

## Rationalism vs. Empiricism

|Dimension|Rationalism|Empiricism|
|---|---|---|
|**Primary Knowledge Source**|Reason, innate ideas, intellectual intuition|Sensory experience, observation|
|**A Priori Knowledge**|Extensive (mathematics, metaphysics, logic)|Minimal or none|
|**A Posteriori Knowledge**|Secondary|Primary and foundational|
|**Innate Ideas**|Affirmed|Denied (_tabula rasa_)|
|**Role of Senses**|Unreliable or derivative|Sole epistemic foundation|
|**Role of Reason**|Constitutive of genuine knowledge|Purely instrumental / organising|
|**Classical Proponents**|Descartes, Leibniz, Spinoza, Kant*|Locke, Berkeley, Hume, Mill|
|**Classical Period**|1600 – 1800 CE|1690 – 1900 CE|
|**Modern Heir**|Chomskyan nativism, modal rationalism|Logical positivism, naturalized epistemology|
|**PhilPapers 2020 (A Priori)**|~71 % accept some a priori knowledge|~17 % deny all a priori knowledge|

Kant synthesized both traditions, arguing that the mind imposes _a priori_ **forms** (space, time) and **categories** (causality) upon empirical **content** — 
his "Copernican Revolution" in epistemology (1781).

### Key Epistemological Distinctions

| Category                   | Independent of Experience? | Modality   | Example                                | Key Figure    |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------- | ---------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------- |
| **A Priori**               | Yes                        | Necessary  | "All bachelors are unmarried"          | Kant (1781)   |
| **A Posteriori**           | No                         | Contingent | "Water boils at 100 °C at 101.325 kPa" | Hume (1748)   |
| **Synthetic A Priori**     | Yes (Kant's claim)         | Necessary  | "7 + 5 = 12" as non-analytic           | Kant (1781)   |
| **Necessary A Posteriori** | No                         | Necessary  | "Water = H₂O"                          | Kripke (1980) |

## Skeptical Challenges

Skepticism questions whether knowledge is possible universally (global skepticism) or in specific domains (local skepticism).

### Taxonomy of Skeptical Positions

|Type|Scope|Paradigm Argument|Key Figure|Approx. Period|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|**Global / Cartesian**|All knowledge|Evil Demon; Dream Argument|Descartes (1641)|17th c. CE|
|**External World**|Physical reality|Brain-in-a-Vat|Putnam (1981)|20th c. CE|
|**Pyrrhonian**|Suspension of all judgment|Equipollence of opposing arguments|Pyrrho (c. 360 BCE)|4th c. BCE|
|**Academic**|Strong knowledge only|Probable opinion is attainable|Arcesilaus (c. 268 BCE)|3rd c. BCE|
|**Humean / Inductive**|Empirical generalisation|Problem of Induction|Hume (1748)|18th c. CE|
|**Moral Epistemology**|Ethical knowledge|Error Theory / Non-cognitivism|Mackie (1977)|20th c. CE|

### Anti-Skeptical Responses

|Response|Core Strategy|Proponent|Est. Endorsement|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Externalism**|Knowledge does not require reflective access to one's justification|Goldman, Nozick|~35 %|
|**Contextualism**|"Knows" is context-sensitive; skeptical standards are not binding in ordinary contexts|DeRose, Lewis|~26 %|
|**Transcendental Argument**|Knowledge is a precondition for the coherence of skeptical inquiry itself|Kant, Strawson|~20 %|
|**Moorean Common-Sense**|Direct affirmation: "Here is a hand" — the denial is less certain than the commonsense claim|G.E. Moore (1925)|~15 %|
|**Pragmatist Dissolution**|Skeptical "doubts" are not genuine, action-guiding doubts|Peirce, Wittgenstein|~10 %|

### Hume's Problem of Induction

The **Principle of Induction** may be stated:

If event-type E occurred in n observed cases, then P(E at case n+1)>0.5 
Hume's dilemma: justifying this principle requires either:

1. **A priori reasoning** → Cannot establish contingent empirical regularities (would make induction analytic).
2. **Inductive reasoning** → Viciously circular (presupposes the principle to be justified).

#### Proposed Solutions to the Problem of Induction

| Solution                   | Core Claim                                                              | Proponent                            |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| **Pragmatic Vindication**  | Induction is the optimal strategy even without guarantee                | Reichenbach (1938)                   |
| **Falsificationism**       | Science proceeds by deductive falsification, not induction              | Popper (_Logik der Forschung_, 1934) |
| **Bayesian Updating**      | Prior probabilities are updated via Bayes' Theorem; no infinite regress | Ramsey, de Finetti, Howson           |
| **Dissolving the Problem** | Demanding justification for induction is a category error               | Strawson (1952)                      |

#### Bayesian Formalism

P(Hypothesis ∣ Evidence)=P(Evidence ∣ Hypothesis) × P(Hypothesis) × P(Evidence)

Where:

- P(Hypothesis) = prior probability before evidence
- P(Evidence ∣ Hypothesis) = likelihood
- P(Hypothesis ∣ Evidence) = posterior probability after evidence


## Epistemic Virtues

Virtue Epistemology, developed systematically from ~1980 onward 
(Sosa, Zagzebski, Greco), reframes the unit of epistemological analysis from _belief-properties_ to _agent-properties_.

### Core Thesis

Knowledge(agent,P)  ≡  TrueBelief(agent,P)  ∧  CreditableToIntellectualVirtue(agent) 

More precisely (Zagzebski, _Virtues of the Mind_, 1996):

Knowledge=ActOfIntellectualVirtue  ∧  SuccessAttributableToVirtue 

### Two Schools Within Virtue Epistemology

| School                                    | Primary Focus                                     | Knowledge Defined As                                   | Representative Thinker               |
| ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| **Reliabilist VE** (Faculty Epistemology) | Reliable faculties: perception, memory, intuition | Belief produced by a reliable, truth-conducive faculty | Sosa (1980–2007)                     |
| **Responsibilist VE**                     | Intellectual character traits and motivations     | Belief formed from virtuous intellectual character     | Zagzebski (1996), Montmarquet (1993) |
| **Hybrid / Other**                        | —                                                 | —                                                      | Greco (2010)                         |

### Catalogue of Epistemic Virtues

|Virtue|Description|Opposing Vice|Domain|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Intellectual Humility**|Accurate calibration of one's epistemic limitations|Arrogance, overconfidence|Self-assessment|
|**Open-Mindedness**|Genuine willingness to revise beliefs upon evidence|Dogmatism, closed-mindedness|Belief revision|
|**Intellectual Courage**|Readiness to defend well-supported but unpopular views|Epistemic cowardice, sycophancy|Social epistemology|
|**Thoroughness (Diligence)**|Adequate inquiry before belief formation|Epistemic laziness, hasty judgment|Inquiry conduct|
|**Intellectual Honesty**|Accurate representation of evidence and reasoning|Deception, self-deception|Communication|
|**Curiosity**|Intrinsic motivation to seek knowledge|Intellectual apathy|Motivation|
|**Carefulness**|Avoidance of unwarranted conclusions|Recklessness|Reasoning quality|
|**Epistemic Autonomy**|Forming beliefs through one's own rational processes|Epistemic dependence, credulity|Agency|
