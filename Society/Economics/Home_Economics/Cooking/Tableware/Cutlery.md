## #has_/text_of_/abstract 

> **Cutlery** (also referred to as silverware, flatware, or tableware) includes any hand implement 
> used in preparing, serving, and especially eating food in Western culture. 
> A person who makes or sells cutlery is called a cutler.
>
> While most cutlers were historically men, women could be cutlers too; Agnes Cotiller was working as a cutler in London in 1346, and training a woman apprentice, known as Juseana.
>
> The city of Sheffield in England has been famous for the production of cutlery since the 17th century and a train â€“ the Master Cutler â€“ running from Sheffield to London was named after the industry. Bringing affordable cutlery to the masses, stainless steel was developed in Sheffield in the early 20th century.
>
> 
>
> The major items of cutlery in Western culture are the knife, fork and spoon. These three implements first appeared together on tables in Britain in the Georgian era. In recent times, hybrid versions of cutlery have been made combining the functionality of different eating implements, including the spork (spoon / fork), spife (spoon / knife), and knork (knife / fork). The sporf or splayd combines all three.
>
